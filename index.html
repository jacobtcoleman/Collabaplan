COLLABAPLAN

Group Travel Planning Application

Families and groups of friends go through an extraordinary amount of time, work, and research, and communicate through many different apps in order to plan a vacation together, and this difficulty grows exponentially as the number of travelers increases.

The solution is to merge communication, research, and planning all into a single mobile application, streamlining the entire process and eliminating many loose ends.

User Research

In the first week, myself and a partner created a research plan to explore the topic of travel, and we created several proto personas to represent four different family members, all with different needs and goals for their ideal vacation.

During our research, we conducted a total of six one-on-one interviews (each interview was scripted and recorded), as well as a survey consisting of 14 check box and short answer questions. We arranged our data from the interviews into an affinity map, categorized the individual data points, and explored our survey data through charts and graphs.


The largest segment of users spend   more than six months planning their vacations.



Definition & Synthesis

From what I had gathered from our research, I developed three separate personas for three separate familes, all in different stages and situations in life, and for each of these personas, a user insight and a problem statement.

I compared and contrasted these personas, insights, and problem statements, and moved forward in the design thinking process with the problem statement that was most supported by our research.




I explored multiple personas, insights, and problem statements, and continued my work by exploring the statement that was most supported by my research.



Ideation

Ideation began with a quick brainstorming exercise - a mind map. After that, I expanded upon my first thoughts into data points of likes, wants, and wishes, and from there I categorized those points into similar wants and needs. I chose the most important ten features and arranged them into a feature prioritization matrix to determine which features were most important.

Planning together and collectively voting on activity choices
were the most important features to develop.

These features were refined into a more defined idea with use of the Value Proposition Canvas. These featured were aligned with my user persona by creating a detailed user scenario and storyboard for the concept, along with a comprehensive user journey map.

Prototyping

For me, the beginning of any idea starts with sketching on paper with pens, pencils, and markers. I find it the most natural way to develop a concept, so I laid out every screen in the first prototype in this fashion, and each was scanned and added to my Miro board for the project.

Once digitized, I made quick work of a low-fidelity prototype in Adobe XD. Next, I introduced a single color and further developed the UI, and then linked each screen into a clickable prototype.

I continued to develop this V1 prototype, adding screens and refining the UI, until it had almost tripled in size and truly became V2, the version that I user tested.

It was essential to have a cohesive aesthetic for the UI, one that was intuitive and easy to digest. The blue, white, and red color scheme was selected for its high contrast and visibility, as well as its commonality.
